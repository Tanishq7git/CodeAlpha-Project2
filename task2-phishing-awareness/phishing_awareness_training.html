Phishing Awareness Training

A briefing on how phishing attacks work, how to spot them in the wild, and the habits that keep you — and everyone in your inbox — safe.

§01 — Briefing

What you're up against

Phishing is a social engineering attack, not a technical one. Attackers don't need to break your systems — they just need one person to click, reply, or hand over a password. It arrives by email, text, or a fake login page, and it is almost always disguised as something routine: a password reset, an invoice, a message from your boss.

This module walks through two annotated exhibits, the tactics behind them, a field guide of habits that stop most attacks cold, and a short instinct test at the end.

§02 — Exhibit A

Anatomy of a phishing email

Tap each numbered marker to see why it's a red flag.

EXHIBIT A — INBOX SCREENSHOT
From
Account Security <security-alert@paypa1-support.com>
Subject
⚠ Urgent: Your account will be suspended in 24 hours

Dear Customer,
We detected unusual activity on your account. Verify your identity within 24 hours or your account will be permanently suspended.

Verify My Account →

Thank you,
PayPal Security Team

Spoofed domain. "paypa1-support.com" uses the digit "1" instead of the letter "l", and it isn't PayPal's real domain at all. Always read sender addresses character by character.
Manufactured urgency. A hard deadline and a threat ("suspended in 24 hours") is designed to make you act before you think.
Generic greeting. A real account-holder message usually uses your name. "Dear Customer" scales to millions of targets at once.
Vague threat, vague ask. "Verify your identity" via an emailed link is a request legitimate companies rarely make unprompted.
Mismatched destination. The button text says "Verify My Account," but hovering over a real version of this link would show it points to a completely different, unfamiliar domain — not paypal.com.
§03 — Exhibit B

Anatomy of a fake website

The link from Exhibit A leads here. Tap the markers.

EXHIBIT B — BROWSER CAPTURE
Address
🔒 https://www.paypa1-secure-login.net/verify

Domain reads "paypa1-secure-login.net" — a padlock icon is present — the page asks for your full card number and PIN to "confirm identity."

Look-alike domain. Extra words like "secure-login" are added specifically to sound trustworthy. The real base domain is what's directly before the first single slash — and here, it isn't paypal.com.
The padlock lied — sort of. HTTPS only means the connection is encrypted. It says nothing about who owns the site. Attackers get free certificates too.
Over-collection. Legitimate "verification" almost never needs your full card number and PIN together. That combination is only useful for theft.
§04 — Dossier

Social engineering tactics

The technical wrapper changes constantly. The psychological lever behind it rarely does.

MO — Urgency

Urgency & Fear

A deadline, a threat, or a locked account. Pressure is designed to skip your instinct to double-check.

MO — Authority

Authority Impersonation

Posing as IT, HR, a regulator, or an executive. Most people hesitate to question someone who outranks them.

MO — Curiosity

Curiosity Bait

"You were tagged in a photo." Intriguing, personal, low-effort to click — which is exactly the point.

MO — Reciprocity

Reciprocity Trap

A small "gift," refund, or favor arrives first, lowering your guard before the real ask comes in.

MO — Trust

Brand Impersonation

Cloned logos, colors, and footer text copied from a real company to borrow its credibility.

MO — Scarcity

Scarcity Pressure

"Offer expires in 10 minutes" or "only 2 spots left" — forcing a decision before it can be checked.

§05 — Field Guide

Standing protocol

Tap a line once you've made it a habit.

0 / 8 protocols reviewed
P-01
Check the sender's actual email address, not just the display name.
P-02
Hover over links before clicking — compare the shown text to the real destination.
P-03
Be suspicious of urgency, threats, or artificial deadlines pressuring immediate action.
P-04
Never enter credentials or payment details through a link in an unsolicited message — type the URL yourself instead.
P-05
Watch for generic greetings, spelling errors, and mismatched branding.
P-06
Verify unusual requests — wire transfers, gift cards, password resets — through a separate, known channel.
P-07
Turn on multi-factor authentication everywhere it's offered.
P-08
Report suspicious messages to IT or security instead of just deleting them — it protects others too.
§06 — Case Files

From the field

Composite scenarios drawn from common phishing patterns.

Case 014

The Wire Transfer

A finance employee received a message that appeared to come from the company's CEO, requesting an urgent, confidential wire transfer to a new vendor account. The tone was authoritative and time-sensitive, discouraging questions.

Lesson — Verify unusual financial requests through a separate channel, every time, no exceptions.
Case 022

The Password Reset

Staff received a notice that their mailbox was full and would be locked unless they "verified" their login within hours. The link led to a cloned page nearly identical to the real one. Employees who entered credentials had their accounts used to send further phishing emails to contacts.

Lesson — "Verify your account now" is one of the most reused phishing templates. Navigate to services directly instead of clicking through.
Case 031

The Curious Click

An employee received a message about being tagged in a photo by someone claiming to be a coworker. Clicking led to a fake login page built to harvest credentials.

Lesson — Curiosity bait works because it feels harmless. Treat surprising notifications with the same caution as urgent ones.
